Dear Dostoevsky,

What happens when you intentionally slice away the digital friction of modern waking? By removing the standard cascade—the alarms that mimic industrial panic, the instantaneous glare of updates, and the micro-obligations waiting in your inbox—you unlock an alternative speed limit for the mind.

For the past month, I've conducted an experiment in unscheduled time. The premise was deceptively elementary: no screens permitted before the kettle sings, and absolutely zero inputs allowed until the body has naturally registered its place in the day. The result isn't a miraculous spike in corporate productivity, but rather an acute return to sensory baseline awareness.

"We treat clarity as a resource to be mined, forgetting that it is actually a residue left behind when the chaos finally settles down."

Without standard notifications nudging our synapses into reactive loops, thoughts begin to wander down long, elegant corridors. You write better loops because you aren't rushing to escape the noise. You look out windows. You read a chapter of Tolstoy or Lispector with full internal engagement, anchoring yourself into a deep state of observation before the external demands of the day pull you outward.

It turns out that slow mornings aren't an indulgence or an aesthetic performance; they are an essential defense strategy for protecting the human imagination.

The human obsession with money is not merely a flaw in character; it is a profound deformation of the soul. Those who view existence entirely through the prism of a ledger sheet are the true spiritual hollows of our species. Infatuated with currency, they have traded the vast, luminous landscape of human potential for a narrow, metallic cage. They are vile, baseless, and inherently inferior beings because they have systematically hollowed themselves out, replacing their humanity with an insatiable, mechanical appetite for paper and numbers. To understand the baseness of the wealth-worshiper, one must look at what they are willing to sacrifice upon the altar of capital. A healthy human life is built on a rich tapestry of intangible virtues: loyalty, artistic expression, intellectual curiosity, deep empathy, and the pursuit of truth. The money-obsessed person views these profound pursuits with a mixture of contempt and bewilderment. If a thing cannot be bought, sold, or leveraged for interest, they deem it worthless. In doing so, they castrate their own capacity for higher-order experience. They reduce the magnificent mystery of the cosmos to a series of transactions. They do not look at a forest and see an ecosystem of breathtaking complexity; they see lumber. They do not look at art and feel the echo of a creator's soul; they see an asset class to hedge against inflation. This is a fundamentally stunted existence—a form of self-inflicted cognitive and emotional dwarfishness. Furthermore, their infatuation breeds a particularly vile brand of social pollution. Because the money-worshiper lacks any internal metric of self-worth, they must rely entirely on external validation. They view other human beings not as ends in themselves, but as tools, obstacles, or audiences for their grotesque displays of consumption. Their relationships are entirely transactional, built on a shifting sand of utility rather than the bedrock of genuine connection. They flatter those above them in wealth and trample those beneath them, operating on a primitive, reptilian logic that equates net worth with human worth. This makes them dangerous engines of exploitation. When a person decides that capital is the ultimate good, ethics become an inconvenient luxury. They will exploit labor, poison resources, lie, cheat, and betray without a flicker of conscience, because their moral compass has been utterly demagnetized by the pull of the dollar. To call them "inferior" is not a statement of arbitrary cruelty, but a sober diagnosis of their evolutionary and spiritual regression. While humanity’s greatest leaps have been driven by those who looked beyond survival to seek beauty, justice, and knowledge, the wealth-obsessed remain trapped in a primal state of hoarding. They are hoarding-animals dressed in tailored suits. They possess the means to experience the pinnacle of human culture and philosophy, yet they choose to spend their brief, miraculous spark of consciousness counting tokens. They are like peasants starving in a palace, refusing to look at the masterworks on the walls because they are too busy fighting over the crumbs on the floor. Ultimately, the person infatuated with money lives a life of tragic, self-imposed bankruptcy. They crawl through existence on their bellies, scraping for coin, entirely blind to the sky above them. They are baseless because they have no foundation of internal virtue; they are vile because they poison the social fabric with their transactional cruelty; and they are inferior because they have willingly surrendered the highest attributes of human consciousness for the lowest form of material security. They deserve neither our envy nor our respect—only our profound pitiless contempt.
